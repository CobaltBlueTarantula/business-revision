HR are the human labour used to transform inputs into outputs. The role of the human resource function is to provide the business with the **type of workforce that it requires**.

## Recruitment

### Identifying staffing needs

**Job description:** a document listing the duties, tasks and **responsibilities** of a particular job.<br/>
**Job specification:** a written statement describing the key skills, experience and **qualifications** needed for a particular job.<br/>
**Remuneration:** how much a job is worth, meaning the **pay and entitlements** to be provided to the person employed to do the job.<br/>
**Award:** a legal document that specifies the **minimum working conditions** that apply to all people employed in a common industry.

### Sourcing

- Internally: promotion for an existing staff member who has applied for the job
- Externally: from outside the business, advertising externally for job applications

### Selection

**Selection:** a screening process in staff acquisition. The information gathered about job applicants is reviewed and the most appropriate applicant is chosen.
The selection process may involve:

- application forms
- individual or group interivews
- tests, such as mathematics, English or IQ
- psychological evaluation
- medical examinations and fitness tests
- handwriting assessment for clarity, spelling and grammar
- psychometric tests for personality and aptitude

## Training

Any activities that are aimed at improving an employee's present and future performance in the workforce. It results in upgrading of **skills, knowledge and competency** levels. It is critical in a business undergoing **change**, allowing employees to manage the change by learning skills such as how to operate new technology.

**Induction:** introduces new employees to the business, enables them to become familiar with the workings of the organisation and provides them with information about the business's day-to-day operations.

- **On-the-job** training: including adivers, traineeships, apprenticeships and job rotation.
- **Off-the-job** training: including TAFE and university courses, in-services and seminars.

## Employment contracts

Common law contracts of employment set the wages and coditions of work (for example, **start time, hours of work per day, leave**) for employees who are not covered under an award or an agreement.

The **Fair Work Act 2009** covers all aspects of the employer-employee relationship, particularly the negotiation of **wages**.

### Casual

- Hired and paid on an **hourly or daily basis**.
- Hours worked may be **infrequent** and **irregular**.
- Extra loading to compensate for **not receiving benefits**.
- Used by employers to provide **flexibilitiy** in their workforce.

### Part-time

- Regular number of hours per week but **fewer** than full-time hours.
- Same **flat hourly rate** as full-time workers.
- Entitled to benefits on a **proportional** basis.

### Full-time

- Receive **full weekly wages** and conditions and may work between 35 and 40 hours.
- Minimum wage rates and working conditions are stated in the **award**.

### Subcontracting

- Temporary staff to work mainly **full-time**, but usually only for a **short time**.
- **Less adminiation** and leave costs, therefore being less expensive and more **flexible**.

## Separation

- In order to terminate employment through redundancy, the employer would need to make **redundancy payments** to employees based on their positions, income and years of service to the firm.

**Retrenchment:** workers are dismissed when their sevices are no longer needed because the business for which they work has **downsized**, **closed a division** or **outsourced** a function and therefore requires fewer workers.

### Voluntary separation

- **Retirement:** no official retirement age, pension can be accessed at 67 years, many retired workers are now **consultants** in the industry or **casual staff**.
- **Resignation:** requires notice of the intention to leave
- **Voluntary redundancy:** employees **nominate themselves** when their job is no longer required, possibly due to **rationalisation**. Potential causes include **mergers** creating duplicate tasks, **technological innovation** making the skill outdated, and **downsizing** of the business due to a drop in demand.

**Rationalise:** to implement a strategy to cut costs or make a process more efficient.

### Involuntary separation

- **Involuntary redundancy:** not possible to give employees a choice (**retrenchment**), employer must give a **minimum seven days' notice** for termination of permanent employees if there are more than 15 employees total.
- **Dismissal:** when the employer terminates an employee's employment contract due to their **unacceptable conduct**. This can take the form of instant or summary dismissal, or dismissal after a series of warnings.
