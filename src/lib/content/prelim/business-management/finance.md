**Financial accounting:** The internal accounting process that produces financial statements about the overall financial position of the business.

**Equity finance:** internal sources of finance, provided by the owners in the form of **startup capital** or purchased **shares**.
Also refers to **reinvested** net profit.<br/>
**Debt finance:** funds borrowed from a bank, investor, or other firm. Involves a contractual agreement that specifies the
need to repay the **principal** with **interest**, over a set **period of time**.<br/>
**Non-current assets:** assets that earn revenue for a business over a long period of time, usually greater than 12 months (eg. land, equipment, vehicles).

## Cash flow statement

A financial report that summarises cash flowing into and out of the business over a period of time. **Controlling cashflow** and **maintaining liquidity** is critiacal to a business's financial success. Failure to control cash flow can result in liquidity problems in the short term and **insolvency** in the long term.

**Liquidity:** indicates how much cash is readily available in a busines. Current assets are listed in order of liquidity, which is how easily they can be turned into cash. Savings in the business's bank acccount are the most liquid as they are already cash.<br/>
**Insolvency:** when a business cannot pay all its liabilities (both current and non-current). An insolvent business will most likely fail because creditors will demand repayment and the businesss cannot afford to pay loans without selling assets.

## Income statement

A summary of all revenues and expenses incurred by a business over a period of time, usually a financial year. It is usually used to work out the business's profitability and efficiency.

**Revenue:** all the funds flowing into the business, including: cash and credit sales, payment for services, rent or interest from investments, supplier discounts, dividends on investment shares.<br/>
**Expenses:** all the costs incurred in earning revenue, including wages, marketing costs, and purchase of materials and stock.<br/>
**Bottom line:** the net profit from business operations after all costs have been paid.

### Purposes

- An income statement can be used as an internal monitoring tool by managers, as it tells them whether the business is profitable based on the **bottom line**.
- An income statement also provides evidence of the business's ability to repay loans and interest when requesting additional **debt finance**.

### Gross profit

**Cost of goods sold (COGS) = Opening stock + Purchases - Closing stock**<br/>
**Gross profit = Sales revenue - Cost of goods sold**<br/>
**Net profit = Gross profit - Expenses**

## Balance sheet

A summary statement of the assets a business owns, the liabilities it owes and the qeuity the owners have invested.

**Assets = Liabilities + Owner's equity** (ALOE)

### Assets

**Current assets:** assets that earn revenue for a business in the short term (eg. cash in the bank and accounts receivable). <br/>
**Non-current assets:** assets that earn revenue over a long period of time (eg. land, buildings, equipment, vehicles, patents).

### Liabilities

**Current liabilities:** money that is owed to an external business that will be repaid in the short term (business credit cards, bank overdraft, accounts payable, commercial bills).<br/>
**Non-current liabilities:** debts of a business that are repaid over a long period of time (long-term loan, mortgage, debentures).

### Owner's equity

Represents the net worth of the business, and is the money that the businness 'owes' to its owner. It mainly includes:

- Capital: the money that the owner has invested into the business.
- Retained profits: profit made in previous financial periods and kept for future use rather than being distributed to the owner as dividends.

**Dividend:** the distribution of profits by a business to its shareholders.
