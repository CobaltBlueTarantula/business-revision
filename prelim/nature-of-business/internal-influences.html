Influences in the Business Environment

Internal influences

Products

Businesses need to consider the way they acquire, store and distribute their products depending on their nature. This includes:

  • How will the business produce its product
  • How far is the business from its suppliers
  • Does the business require storage facilities
  • What are the most effective methods of maintaining quality stock
  • How far is the manufacturing plant from stores and outlets
  • How far is the business located from its customers

Location

Retail

  • High visibility to attract passing customers
  • Cost of renting or leasing the factory or shop
  • Location of suppliers
  • Most retail or service businesses will choose locations near their target market
  • Demographic factors of their target market should also be considered (eg. luxury products in affluent areas)
  • Large retailers of consumer goods will require space to display stock

Manufacture

  • Availability and cost of obtaining inputs such as raw materials
  • Distance to markets and its effect on transport costs
  • Transport facilities (roads, railways, ports, airports)
  • Access to water and power
  • Space for future expansion

Proximity to competitors

Positioning near competitors can attract more potential customers to the area if businesses of the same type are close together. As certain locations become known as the place to purchase a particular item, competitors want to tap into the large number of customers.

Proximity to support services

Services provided by other businesses to help a firm perform its core functions. Outsourcing is cheaper and allows the business to focus on its core business functions. These include

  • Delivery between stores or to customers
  • Copying and printing for marketing
  • Legal advice
  • Catering
  • IT support and computer repairs
  • Maintenance and repair services
  • Cleaning of the office or factory
  • Security guards
  • Infrastructure (transport, power, communication)
  • Banking

Resources

Financial resources: the funds required by a business to access other resources and then produce and sell its goods and services. Financial resources may be accessed from owners or financial institutions.

Input resources: goods and services that combine to produce a finished good or service which is then sold to consumers. The cost of inputs is important, including machinery, raw materials and labour.

Human resources: employees are responsible for providing the human effort that transforms inputs into a finished good or service, requiring expertise, skill and knowledge.

Management and business culture

An effective corporate culture is the way staff perform their work, including values and beliefs within a business. A strong culture is one in which employees work together effectively, share the same values and make decisions to meet the business’s objectives.

Business culture: the informal, unwritten rules and procedures followed in a business.
