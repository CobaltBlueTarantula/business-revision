Influences in the Business Environment

External influences

Economic

Economic cycle: the fluctuation of consumer and business spending over a period of time.

DownswingUpswing
Lower disposable income, loss of confidence, reduced consumer spending, decrease in sales, increasing unemployment.Higher income, higher consumer demand, increased spending, increased business profit.

Fiscal policy: government actions, such as the use of taxation and expenditure, that are intended to influence the level of economic activity in Australia. Mainly operates through the Commonwealth Budget.

Monetary policy: actions taken by the Reserve Bank of Australia (the federal government’s bank) to influence the level of interest rates in the Australian economy. An increase in interest rates means higher charges on mortgages, reducing consumers’ disposable income.

Microeconomic reform: policies developed by the government to promote greater competition within a particular industry.

Financial

Debt finance is greatly influenced by the level of interest rates. As interest rates are the costs of borrowing money, increases in interest rate levels may reduce the amount of debt finance undertake by a business. This is because it becomes more expensive to acquire and service the borrowed funds and requires a higher level of profit to make finance worthwhile. The business must earn enough money to be able to make loan repayments.

Equity finance tends to decrease as interest rates increase and share prices fall. This is because more ownership must be sold to raise the same funds, making equity financing unattractive.

  • Equity finance: internal sources of finance, provided by the owners in the form of startup capital or purchased shares. Also refers to reinvested net profit.
  • Debt finance: funds borrowed from a bank, investor, or other firm. Involves a contractual agreement that specifies the need to repay the principal with interest, over a set period of time.

Deregulation: the government’s removal of or reduction in controls and regulations on an industry or sector of the economy or market in order to achieve greater competition.

Competitive situation

Competitive situation is influenced by the number of competitors and the ease with which a business can enter a particular market.

Ease of entry

Ease of entry is based on:

  • Cost of establishing the business (financial resources)
  • Access to materials (availability of inputs)

Market situations

Monopoly: only one firm in the marketplace.
Oligopoly: a limited number of suppliers to the market, meaning there is less pressure on businesses to offer low prices and innovate.

Perfect competition: many small firms sell identical products to many buyers, and no single entity can influence prices, which are determined by supply and demand.
Monopolistic competition: many firms sell products that are similar but differentiated, allowing them some control over pricing. Combines elements of both monopoly and perfect competition.

Institutional

Environment Protection Authority (EPA): enforces NSW government laws regarding protection of the environment.

Australian Taxation Office (ATO): enforces and administers federal government taxation policies and laws. It is the responsibility of the employer to deduct income tax and then forward it to the ATO on the employee’s behalf.

Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC): monitors the operations of financial institutions. Requires private and public companies to release annual reports detailing the business’s financial performance over the past year. Businesses must submit a prospectus to the ASIC before investors can buy shares in the firm. Regulates the reporting of financial data from companies to their shareholders.

Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC): regulates the levels of competition within a range of industries. It aims to promote ethical business behaviour towards competitors and allows lodging complaints against unfair behaviour of competitors. The ACCC is able to penalise businesses that engage in deceptive and misleading conduct or price-fixing (competitors conspiring to set high prices to reduce levels of competition).

NSW Fair Trading: protects the rights of consumers, regarding product safety, refunds, warranties, exchanges and the provision of faulty goods and inadequate services. It also regulates the registration of business names.

Trade unions: organisations that aim to protect and promote the interests and working conditions of employees. They assist employees with workplace disputes and wage negotiations.

Employer associations: organisations that aim to promote the interests of employers within the business environment. They lobby governments to develop policies that benefit employers and consult them on key policy issues.

Australian Securities Exchange (ASX): responsible for acting as a market where investors may buy and sell shares in public companies. Public companies must adivse the ASX about factors that might significantly affect their share price and ensure people cannot use confidential information for financial gain.

Consumer associations: organsiation that are developed to provide informative opinions to consumers on a wide range of issues such as quality and pricing. They are independent and not affiliated with profit-based businesses.

Technological

Also see Using technology in SMEs

Developments in technology result in increased efficiency and productivity. They create opportunities for the development of new products and innovations in existing products.

Effects of technology

Managers must assess the cost of the installation of the technology, its impact on production and the expected profitability generated by the change. Potential costs include:

  • Staff training
  • Time lost while changes are introduced
  • Redundancy of some employees
  • Environmental impacts
  • Community perception

Geographic

Changes to the demographics of Australia’s population have resulted in geographical changes to markets.

Social

Society expects businesses to contribute to the community’s quality of life. Activities can often provide publicity for the businesses involved. Therefore businesses need to be aware of their community’s needs and respond to the community’s changes in culture and taste.

Legal

Government regulations promote fair conduct. This includes laws through the common law judical system.

Political

Privatisation: the process of transferring the ownership of a government business to the private sector by issuing a prospectus and listing the ASX as a public company.
